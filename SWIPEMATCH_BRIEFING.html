01
App overview
// what we are building and why

SwipeMatch is a bilateral swipe-based job matching mobile app built in Flutter. Candidates swipe on jobs, employers swipe on candidates, and Claude AI scores and explains every match. The goal is to replace the outdated form-based job board paradigm (LinkedIn, Indeed, ZipRecruiter) with a mobile-first, dopamine-driven, AI-powered experience that feels like Tinder but delivers LinkedIn-level career outcomes.

$17.5B
Online job market in 2026, growing at 12.9% CAGR. Incumbents use 2005-era UX.
Bilateral swipe
Both sides swipe. Double the DAUs, double the revealed preference data, better AI.
Mobile-first
78% of job searching happens on mobile. LinkedIn was bolted onto mobile. We were born there.

// claude API — never call from flutter directly
Critical rule: Flutter NEVER calls the Claude API directly. All AI calls go through Supabase Edge Functions. This keeps the API key off the device and allows server-side caching and rate limiting.

Flow: Flutter → Supabase Edge Function (Deno/TypeScript) → Claude API → response to Flutter

Psychology engine
// every behavioral mechanic and the science behind it
Variable reward schedule
Skinner (1957). Slot machine mechanic — users don't know if the next card is their dream job. This unpredictable reward schedule is the most powerful driver of compulsive behavior ever documented. Every swipe is a micro-gamble. Implement by never showing a preview of the next card.
→ Used by: slot machines, Tinder, Instagram feed
Loss aversion (FOMO)
Kahneman (1979). Fear of missing a match is more motivating than hope of finding one. Implement via: cards expire after 24h, "3 employers viewed your profile today" push at 6pm, "This role closes in 4 hours" expiry chip on job cards. The countdown is not decorative — it is the mechanic.
→ Used by: Booking.com, Snapchat, stock trading apps
Identity-anchored streaks
Duolingo-proven. Streaks shift the behavior from external (finding a job) to internal identity ("I am a person who shows up every day"). Identity is stickier than goals. Track last_active_date, show 🔥 N in bottom nav. Missing a streak feels like a personal failure, not just a missed task.
→ Used by: Duolingo, Snapchat, GitHub contribution graph
Investment escalation (Ikea effect)
Norton et al. (2012). Things we build, we value. The more a user builds their profile (video pitch, portfolio, skills, culture tags), the more invested they feel and the harder it is to leave. Every profile improvement is both a sunk cost and a future asset. The profile completion bar makes this visible and exploits it continuously.
→ Used by: LinkedIn profile strength, dating apps
Social proof loops
"847 people in your city applied to similar roles this week." "Your profile is in the top 12% of candidates in your field." Anchoring to peer behavior creates competitive urgency without explicit pressure. People calibrate their behavior against visible social proof automatically.
→ Used by: Airbnb, Booking.com, social media follower counts
Reciprocity bias
Cialdini (1984). After the AI gives a candidate free coaching, a salary insight, or a resume analysis, they feel an invisible obligation to give something back — which manifests as upgrading to premium. The free gift creates a debt. Every freemium business that works exploits this. Give generously before asking.
→ Used by: every successful freemium SaaS product

The "aha moment" must happen within 48 hours. The AI must surface at least 1 highly relevant match in the first session. Seed employer side aggressively before launch — a candidate who swipes 10 times and gets no matches will never return. Pre-load 50 employers before opening to candidates.

Daily deck + streak system + push notification at the same time every day (8am for new deck, 6pm for profile views). Anchor the app open to a specific time. Salary transparency boards, insider interview intel, career trajectory maps, skills battle leaderboards — these community-generated data assets can't be exported. The more a user contributes, the more they are locked in. Exit cost becomes enormous. This is the anti-churn moat that LinkedIn cannot replicate quickly.

Even after hiring: passive mode
70% of employed people are open to new roles. Toggle passive_mode = true and they stay in the database, visible to employers, receiving only "exceptional match" notifications. Employers love access to passive candidates. Users love the weekly market pulse. This creates a perpetual pool that makes the network more valuable to everyone.

Risks to plan for
// know these before you build, not after
!
Cold start problem
Without employers, candidates leave. Without candidates, employers leave. Solution: pre-seed 50 committed employers before any public launch. Never open to candidates with an empty shelf. The first session must produce at least one good match or the user is gone forever.
!
AI bias liability
AI matching can perpetuate gender, ethnicity, age, and school bias at scale. Employment discrimination law is strict in most markets. You need explainable AI scores (we already have this — the match_reason JSON), regular bias audits, and legal counsel before scaling to regulated markets like EU or USA.
~
Incumbent response
LinkedIn could add swipe. Indeed could copy the UI. Your moat is: bilateral match data (they can't replicate the signal), brand ("the one that's honest"), community data (salary reports, intel — users won't re-contribute elsewhere), and the ghost tracker (LinkedIn would never build this — employers are their biggest customers).
~
Gamification fatigue
Jobs are high-stakes. Some users will resist treating job search like a game. Balance: the AI value must be real and tangible, not cosmetic. The match explanation, coaching tip, and salary intelligence are genuine utility. The confetti and streaks are the wrapper. Utility first, dopamine second.
